When Should You Use “Whom,” and Does Anyone Care?

Let’s be honest: “whom” has a reputation. It’s the formal, stiff cousin of “who” — the kind of word that makes even native speakers pause and wonder if they sound too fancy or just plain awkward. So, when should you actually use whom? And more importantly… does anyone really care?

The Traditional Rule: Who’s the Subject, Whom’s the Object

Here’s the grammar 101 version:

Use who when you’re talking about the subject of a sentence — the doer of the action.

Use whom when it’s the object of a verb or a preposition — the receiver of the action.

For example:

“Who is coming to the party?” (Who is doing the coming.)

“To whom should I address the letter?” (Whom is the object of the preposition to.)

But Real People Speak Differently. In everyday conversation, most people skip whom entirely. Instead of “To whom should I address the letter?” you’ll probably hear “Who should I address the letter to?” That’s perfectly fine in casual speech and even in informal writing. Native speakers have been bending and breaking this rule for centuries, Shakespeare was a un-apologetic rule breaker, he frequently used sentence fragments and inverted word order.

When Does “Whom” Still Matter?

If you’re writing a job application, giving a presentation, or drafting a formal email, using whom correctly can make you sound polished and professional. It shows you know your stuff, it's the little umbrella in your Grammar cocktail. Plus, it can prevent confusion in tricky sentences where who/whom isn’t obvious.

Handy Tricks to Know

1: In formal speaking or writing, a preposition should never end a sentence. Instead, it's best to lead the sentence with it, resulting in a natual flow in to "whom".

"To who is this email going?"

If that sounds awkward or wonky, congradulations, you already get it.

2: Using "whom" will almost always require the sentence to be rebuilt with a diferent structure.

"Who is this email being sent to?" (perfectly acceptable casual english) becomes

"To whom is this email being sent?" (now we're getting fancy!)

3: Not sure if you need who or whom? Try swapping in “he” or “him”:

If “he” fits, use who

If “him” fits, use whom

For example:

“Who/Whom called you?” → “He called you.” → Use who.

“You called who/whom?” → “You called him.” → Use whom.

Bottom Line: Don’t Sweat It Too Much, if you accidentally say who instead of whom, you won’t confuse anyone and you won’t be thrown out of the grammar club. But if you want to impress in formal settings or just enjoy playing with language, mastering whom is a neat trick up your sleeve.

And as always, keep it currious.
