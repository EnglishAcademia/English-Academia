The Many Flavors of Verbs

2026-01-03

Are all verbs created equally?

Verbs do a lot of heavy lifting in English. They tell us what is happening, what is changing,

and sometimes what is not changing at all. Without verbs, sentences would just sit there

like unlabeled boxes. Grammatically correct, perhaps, but not terribly useful.

The tricky part is that not all verbs behave the same way. Some are energetic and active.

Some are calm and stationary. Some just quietly connect ideas without doing much themselves.

Understanding these differences makes English suddenly feel less arbitrary and far more logical.

Think of verbs as having flavors.

Action Verbs: The Ones That Get Things Done

Action verbs are exactly what they sound like. They describe actions. Things happening in the real world or in the mind.

She runs every morning.

They built a house.

He forgot her name.

We discussed the problem.

Some of these actions are visible. Running, building, opening. Others happen internally.

Thinking, deciding, remembering. Both count. If something is happening, even quietly,

you are probably dealing with an action verb.

Action verbs are also very comfortable in continuous tenses.

She is running late.

We were discussing the issue.

I am thinking about your idea.

If a verb sounds natural with “is” or “are” plus “-ing,” it is almost certainly an action verb.

So far, so straightforward.

Stative Verbs: The Ones That Prefer to Sit Still

Stative verbs describe states, not actions. They tell us how things are, not what they are doing.

These often relate to:

thoughts and beliefs: know, believe, understand

emotions: love, hate, prefer

possession: have, own, belong

senses: see, hear, smell

Examples:

I know the answer.

She loves her job.

We own this house.

He believes the story.

Here is the key idea. Stative verbs usually do not like continuous forms.

These sentences sound wrong to most native speakers:

I am knowing the answer.

She is loving her job.

We are owning this house.

Why? Because these verbs describe conditions that are already true. You do not actively

“do” knowing or owning in the same way you do running or building. They simply exist.

This is where many learners get frustrated, because English immediately complicates things.

When Verbs Change Their Mind

Some verbs can be action verbs or stative verbs depending on how they are used. Context decides.

Compare:

I think you are right.

I am thinking about your suggestion.

In the first sentence, think describes a belief. That is stative. In the second, it describes

a mental process. That is an action.

Another classic pair:

The soup tastes good.

She is tasting the soup.

Same verb. Different flavor.

English does this a lot. It bends its own patterns for nuance and expression. Once you understand

the difference between action and state, these shifts start to make sense instead of feeling random.

Linking Verbs: The Quiet Middlemen

Linking verbs do not describe action or state in the usual way. Their job is to connect the

subject to information about the subject.

The most common linking verb is be:

She is tired.

The sky was gray.

Others include:

seem

appear

become

feel

look

sound

taste

smell

Examples:

He seems confident.

The plan became complicated.

The music sounds beautiful.

A helpful test is this. If the verb is followed by an adjective or a noun that describes the

subject, you are probably looking at a linking verb.

Compare:

She looks happy.

She looks at the painting.

In the first sentence, looks links the subject to a description. In the second, it describes

an action. Same word. Different job.

So Did McDonald’s Make a Grammar Mistake?

This brings us to one of the most famous verb questions in modern English.

“I’m lovin’ it.”

Technically speaking, love is a stative verb. By the traditional rule, it should not appear

in the continuous form. So did McDonald’s get it wrong?

Not really.

In this case, love is being treated as a temporary, emotional experience, not a permanent state.

The slogan is not saying “I love this forever as a deep emotional truth.” It is saying “I am enjoying this right now.”

Native speakers do this all the time:

I’m loving this weather.

She’s really loving her new job.

Is it formal? No. Is it natural? Very much so.

This is a perfect example of why understanding grammar matters more than memorizing rules.

McDonald’s was not ignorant of the rule. They were bending it deliberately for tone, immediacy, and voice.

That is not bad grammar. That is expressive grammar.

Why This Matters More Than You Think

Recognizing verb types helps with:

correct tense choice

natural phrasing

exam accuracy

understanding why something sounds wrong, not just that it does

When a learner writes “I am knowing him for five years,” the problem is not vocabulary.

It is verb flavor. Once know is identified as stative, the correct form becomes obvious.

I have known him for five years.

Grammar stops feeling like a list of exceptions and starts acting like a system.

Final Bite

Action verbs move.

Stative verbs rest.

Linking verbs connect.

Once you see what a verb is trying to do, the sentence usually falls into place.

And when English breaks its own patterns, it usually does so for a reason.

The trick is knowing when a rule is being broken on purpose.

That is when grammar gets interesting.

If you enjoyed this one, stick around. There are plenty more flavors to sample.
